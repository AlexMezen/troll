Kristiansand is one of the most beautiful cities in Norway, located on the southern coast where the Baltic Sea meets the North Sea. Founded in 1641 by King Christian IV, the city has preserved its charm and traditional Scandinavian character.


The city center, known as Kvadraturen, is a unique district built on a rectangular street grid. The narrow streets, lined with houses featuring colorful wooden facades, are perfect for strolling. This is where most of the city's cultural landmarks are concentrated:


  • The neo-Gothic Kristiansand Cathedral. Its high spires are visible from afar, and inside, a unique atmosphere of peace and tranquility awaits.
  • The 17th-century Christiansholm Fortress, built to protect the city from naval attacks.
  • Fiskebrygga – a place where you can taste the freshest seafood dishes. In summer, the area is lively with cozy outdoor cafes, and boat trips depart from the quay.
  • One of the city's oldest areas, Posebyen, where wooden houses from the 18th and 19th centuries have been preserved. Walking here feels like stepping back in time, surrounded by the quiet charm of old streets.
  • Kristiansand Zoo and Amusement Park – the largest in Norway and a true pride of the city. It is home to animals from around the world, and nearby, there is a water park and attractions for the whole family.

    • As you wander along the sandy beaches and central streets of the city, you will feel the soul of Southern Norway – calm yet bright and vibrant.


    But what truly makes this city special is its secret side. In fact, Kristiansand is home not only to humans but also to small trolls who have been part of the city's life for centuries.


    The trolls are equal citizens of the city, just like humans. They live in crooked houses hidden among Kvadraturen’s narrow streets or under various bridges. Their homes are distinguished by slightly smaller doors and windows that glow with warm light even at night. The trolls walk the same streets as people but often remain invisible – unless you know where to look.


    These small inhabitants not only live peacefully alongside people but also help them. For example, they take care of small, overlooked corners of the city: repairing broken benches in parks, watering flowers in flower beds, or clearing tiny paths in green areas.


    So if one day you stroll through Kristiansand, take a peek into the most unexpected corners – perhaps there, the trolls are holding a meeting or painting the tiny doors of their houses. And remember, this city thrives not only thanks to its human residents but also because of the small, kind trolls who are always nearby, even if you can’t see them.
